## Overview

This case study explores the design and research process behind an augmented reality navigation application built for UCSD's campus.

## Problem

New students at large universities often experience significant wayfinding anxiety. Traditional maps are hard to parse in real-time, and blue-dot GPS navigation doesn't translate well to sprawling pedestrian campuses.

## Research

I conducted 12 semi-structured interviews with first-year students and observed wayfinding behaviour across 3 campus zones. Key insights:

- Students relied on landmarks, not building names
- Anxiety peaked when walking between buildings for the first time
- Phone-down behaviour was preferred — students didn't want to stare at a screen

## Design Process

Using insights from research, I developed three prototype directions:

1. **Landmark anchors** — AR arrows tied to recognisable visual features
2. **Breadcrumb trail** — a subtle path overlay on the ground plane
3. **Ambient cues** — subtle directional audio combined with minimal visual hints

## Outcome

The breadcrumb trail approach tested 40% better on task completion and rated highest on confidence scores. Final prototype was validated with 20 participants in a live campus setting.

## Reflections

This project taught me how physical context shapes digital interaction. The most technically capable solution (audio cues) was the least trusted — users needed visual confirmation.
